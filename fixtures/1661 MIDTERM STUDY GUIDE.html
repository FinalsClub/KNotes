Preface and Introduction (Hollinger and Capper)

Most of the selections in AIT are of the genres classically associated with purposive discourse: sermon, address, letter, treatise, and essay. They respond to issues that have “persistently generated extensive intellectual discussion.” Most of these selections concern the “theoretical basis for religious, scientific, artistic, political, social, and economic practice.” Hollinger believes that, “the United States is above a polity” and that the public culture of America has “often been caught up in the distinctive ethos of Protestant Christianity” while modern America has been a “peculiarly science-preoccupied civilization.” (xii)

In writing “The Will to Believe” in 1897, William James sought to “vindicate the individual soul’s right to retain aspects of the Protestant heritage not actually proven false by the advancement of secular learning” (3). The documents of Part One of AIT display a “tendency, if not a determination, to ground arguments in naturalistic and humanistic premises” meaning they seek to ground their arguments, even if in support of religious belief, in a rational, scientific, or humanistic authority rather than scriptural or divine authority (3). Moving on to the Gilded Age thinkers, Hollinger believes that it was their hope that “social science might provide a new, solid foundation for organizing and governing their society” (4).

 

“The Fixation of Belief” (Charles Pierce)

In this piece, Hollinger argues that Pierce manages to:

  1. articulate a largely social vision of the scientific enterprise, according to which the community rather than the individual knower is the primary agent of cognitive process

  2. direct the method of science against not only beliefs about nature, but against the entire panorama of inherited beliefs, especially and explicitly religious beliefs

  3. assert that open, honest inquiry is decidedly superior morally to pious loyalty to an inherited faith

  4. argue, in what would eventually be recognized as a “pragmatic” mode, that true belief is simply a habit that can remain stable, a “fixed opinion” (15)

     In “The Fixation of Belief”, Charles Peirce assesses the methods by which people move from states of doubt to states of belief, ultimately endorsing the scientific method. It is historically significant because it casts the Darwinian controversy as “a question of logic” rather than a question of religious belief. In his theory of natural selection, Pierce argues, Darwin is merely applying the statistical method, which is itself objective, to biology.

     Pierce goes on to explain why we draw certain inferences from certain observations:

     That which determines us, from given premises, to draw one inference rather than another, is some habit of mind, whether it be constitutional or aquired. â€¦an inference is regarded as valid or not, without reference to the truth or falsity of its conclusions specially, but according as the habit which determines it is such as to produce true conclusions in general or not. (17)

This habit of mind provides the “guiding principle” of inference in which a “variety of facts are already assumed when the logical question is first asked” such as the fact that “there are such states of mind as doubt and belief—that a passage from one to the other is possible, the object of thought remaining the same, and that this transition is subject to some rules which all minds are alike bound by” (18).

     Pierce believes it is better to believe than to doubt because: “The feelings of believing is a more or less sure indication of there being established in our nature some habit which will determine our actions. Doubt never has such effect” (19). However, doubt, too, is important because doubt “stimulates us to action until it is destroyed” and “the irritation of doubt is the only immediate motive for the struggle to attain belief” (19). In the end, however, Pierce suggests that validity is different from truth, in that an objective process may render a belief valid, but not true:  

     And it is clear that nothing out of the sphere of our knowledge can be our object, for nothing which does not affect the mind can be a motive for a mental effort. The most that can be maintained is, that we seek for a belief that we shall think to be true. 19

Pierce then goes on to articulate four methods of “fixing belief” such that it can withstand a doubt and be acted upon:

  1. the method of tenacity

  2. the method of authority

  3. the a priori method

  4. the scientific method

 

In the “method of tenacity”, one does whatever it takes to shut himself out from all influences that may shake his belief.  This method, however, can hardly work because “unless we make ourselves hermits, we shall necessarily influence each other’s opinions.” This means “that the problem becomes how to fix belief, not in the individual merely” (the scientific method solves this by providing a standard by which the community as a whole can fix belief) (21).

In the “method of authority”, the state creates an institution which shall have for its object “to keep correct doctrines before the attention of the people, to reiterate them perpetually, and to teach them to the young; having at the same time power to prevent contrary doctrines from being taught, advocated, or expressed” (21).

In the “a priori method”, positions are adopted because “their fundamental propositions seemed ‘agreeable to reason’ for those that come up with the positions; in other words, the actions of “natural preferences” are unimpeded and it is hoped that men conversing together will gradually develop beliefs in “harmony with natural causes”. Pierce likens this to the develop meant of taste in art or food.

Considering these methods inadequate for the task of community belief, Pierce calls for a fourth method:

     To satisfy our doubts, therefore, it is necessary that a method should be found by which our beliefs may be caused by nothing human, but by some external permanency—by something upon which our thinking has no effect. (23)

The “scientific method” achieves this because, with it, “the test of whether I am truly following the method is not an immediate appeal to my feelings and purposes, but, on the contrary, itself involves the application of the method” (24). The hypothesis of the scientific method is:

     There are real things, whose characters are entirely independent of our opinions about them; whose realities affect our senses according to regular laws, and, though our sensations are as different as our relations to the objects, yet, by taking advantage of the laws of perception, we can ascertain by reasoning how things really are, and any man, if he have sufficient experience and reason enough about it, will be led to the same conclusion. (23)

Pierce goes on place faith in man’s ability to shed comfortable beliefs with reflection:

     The force of habit will sometimes cause a man to hold on to old beliefs, after he is in a condition to see that they have no sound basis. But reflection upon the state of the case will overcome these habits, and he ought to allow reflection full weight. (25)

Finally, Pierce makes the claim that avoiding the truth is immoral:

     But, above all, let it be considered that what is more wholesome than any particular belief, is integrity of belief; and that to avoid looking into the support of any belief from a fear that it may turn out rotten is quite as immoral as it is disadvantageous. (25)  

      

“Responsibility, Convention, and the Role of Ideas” (Thomas Haskell)

     In this piece, Haskell sets out to explain how various generations can view similar phenomena in very different ways and specifically treats the example of slavery as viewed in modern times and ancient Greek times. In his view, convention places “unrecognized constraints” on the reason and imagination of men. In doing this, Haskell joins the project of Quentin Skinner in urging people to “explore convention’s shadowy domain of unconscious habit, presupposition, prejudgment, and prejudice neither to discredit reason, nor to belabor its shortcomings, but in hopes of strengthening its claims within a more defensible perimeter” (281).

     Haskell argues that one must consider that the concept of “responsibility” as we know it was foreign to the ancients before passing judgment on their treatment of slavery:      

     What is most intriguing about the comparatively short etymological lineage of “responsible” and “responsibility”  is the thought that our conceptions of morality and human agency, in which these terms figure so prominently today, may be less a timeless feature of human nature and more the product of changing historical conditions than is commonly recognized. (283)   

Haskell identifies “cause, intention, state, and response” as the four elements by which both modern and ancient thinking judged responsibility (292). The difference, then, is not so much conceptions of justice, as it is conventions of material existence: “Instead, the difference lies in their comparative inability to imagine any course of action that would bring about the practical implementation of those ideals” (295). For the ancients, slavery was not subject to moral judgments because “its existence seemed necessary and its abolition almost literally unthinkable” (294). In other words, slavery was considered to be a “necessary evil”. What has changed, then, if concepts of justice have not, are the technical-material constraints that rendered slavery “necessary”.  Just like slavery, the technological advances are shifting our the contemporary concept of “responsibility” by expanding the “domain of necessity” or our reach in matters of altruistic intervention—no longer are we only able to save those with whom we are in immediate contact, but advances in transportation and financial transfer have allowed us to save those who live on the other side of the earth. These advances have thus changed convention, and we must always take this into account before making historical judgments.

Norton, Whitman, and Crane

Norton – “American Political Ideas” 1865:

Background: Norton was involved in founding the Nation and North American Review during the Civil War era. Obsessed with European art and culture, became Harvard’s first art history professor. Unlike Whitman, Kloppenburg argued that Norton was not so concerned that new forms of art reach out to ordinary people as much as he believed that American should embrace medieval and renaissance European art. He is the Victorian compared to Whitman the challenger.

Norton argued that American democracy was exceptional because it had a different “essence” than Europe - a new set of guiding political principles under which the “doctrine of privilege” that put the rights of kings, nobles, and clergy above the rest of society was replaced by ideas emphasizing the equality of all men. Sovereignty was derived from within, from the collective will of “the People”. Norton saw the great mass of Americans becoming a second and far more important “controlling agency” in society; an “extra-governmental organization,” surpassing the government itself. In this way for Norton the people formed a moral community held together by a shared moral code or identity. Their political behavior was the process through which they put these morals into action. Norton believed that this self-government could allow American institutions to be perfected and evolve over time with the changing needs of the community.

Writing in 1865, Norton expressed a self-righteous “confidence” in “the perpetual beneficent progress of the principles on which American institutions of government and of society are based,” despite “the evilsâ€¦ mistakes and defects” in American life exposed by the recently concluded Civil War. He went as far as to claim “a perfect commonwealth might here become a reality.” The Civil War did not temper Norton’s confidence because he saw the South as fighting against the ideas of American democracy and not sharing the same moral identity that guided the rest of the American community. “It can not be repeated too often; that this war was a war of ideas.” The South was invested in “the opposite” of the principles of American democracy in “every particular.” He argued that America’s primary task after the war was to “secure” a “moral Union, a civil unity” and victory gave the North an opportunity to impose its moral order – even civilization itself on the South. “The North is civilized, the South is uncivilizedâ€¦ One must take the likeness of the other.”

Whitman – “Democratic Vistas” 1867, 1871:

Background: Whitman (1819 -1892) was a Vicotrian era poet who aspired to become the “voice of ordinary Americans” and wanted art to relate to the average and common man, not just the elite. To quote Kloppenburg, Whitman wanted to “explode conventions” and “become the singer of the song of the new democratic ethos” by creating leading a movement for a new American literature. His work is said to be a direct challenge to Victorian sensibilities.

Whitman stressed that American exceptionalism lay in the future. He believed America was only in “its birth throes.” The war had given Whitman a sense of the “latent” power in American democracy that could be tapped in the future and allow America to “come to dominate the world.” Whitman also espoused the greatness of democratic institutions for giving power to the people and binding people into “a brotherhood,” etcâ€¦, but Whitman did not believe America had yet achieved the “solidarity” necessary to be exceptional, because he argued America’s unique political foundations and institutions were not sufficient to make democracy successful. “I would alarm theâ€¦ readerâ€¦ to the utmost extent, against the prevailing delusion that the establishment of free political institutionsâ€¦ do, of themselves, determine and yield to our experiment of democracy the fruitage of success.” This was because where Norton found a strong moral civic culture at the heart of American democracy, Whitman found “hollowness at heart than at present” in America. He argued that Americans lived “in an atmosphere of hypocrisy throughoutâ€¦ saturated in corruption.” “The underlying principles of the States are not honestly believed in.” “Our New World democracyâ€¦ is, so far, an almost complete failure in social aspects,” etcâ€¦

To overcome this, Whitman argued that it was necessary for America to abandon its reliance on European traditions in art and literature, which “have had their birth in courtsâ€¦ and castle sunshine” and are “are poisonous to the idea of the pride and dignity of the common people, the life blood of democracy,” and create its own tradition of truly American art and literature (especially poetry! (figures)). This was necessary, Whitman argued because literature and art go “deeper” and take “hold in men’s hearts, emotions and belief” and “togetherâ€¦ give more compaction and more moral identity” to America than “all its hitherto politicalâ€¦ experiences.” Whitman’s American art and literature would not be aimed at an elite but at bringing culture to the common people – the “average man” – and give all Americans a universal set of norms and models for behavior to create a distinctly American personality. At the end Whitman argues that an “all-penetrating religiosity” must be at the heart of the new American culture, hands out advice to young people who might want to go into politics, and warns that women might be up to something “revolutionary” like entering into politics and asking for suffrageâ€¦

Crane – “An Episode of War” 1899:

This was tied into the broader themes of the shift from romanticism to realism (compare to the transitions in Kloppenburg’s the art slide show, etc). Crane, who had come up through poverty as a worker in the Bowery and a baseball player, was said to represent a new generation of writers emerging in the late 19th century to challenge Victorian romanticism. Kloppenburg said Crane’s writing represented a “new American naturalism,” in which his characters were not high-minded, high-society types, but dark, more realistic portrayals of everyday American life.

This reading should be easily recognizable because it is in short story prose, etc. It’s only a page and a half so it might be quicker to just read it than read this. The basic plot is: a Civil War lieutenant is distributing coffee among his men when he is suddenly shot in the arm. He is stunned by his wound and the men stare at him in awe before helping him sheath his sword and move away from the line. He leaves the scene of the battle and moves into the rear with his arm hastily bandaged where he passes a general and artillery companies preparing a barrage. He makes his way to a field hospital in an old school house where a doctor amputates his arm. He returns home to his sobbing family and “standing shamefaced amidâ€¦ tears” tries to shrug off the fact he’s missing his arm.

The story is certainly a dark, brooding, realistic portrayal of the Civil War and each paragraph presents a little vignette into the people and environment of the battlefield. Kloppenburg connected the Crane piece back into Whitman’s “Democratic Vistas” by asking us if we think Crane’s writing is the kind of democratic art Whitman was calling for. If you think it is, and I guess that’s a subjective assessment, it might be good to connect Crane back to Whitman if you need to describe the significance of the piece. It is interesting to note that while this is the most vivid civil war piece of the three (James’s “Shaw Oration” and Holmes’s “A Soldier’s Faith”), and Crane’s most famous book, Red Badge of Courage is about the war, Crane, born in 1871, did not have any personal experience with the war.


Sumner and Bellamy:

Historical Background:

Gilded Age America: The gilded age was perceived as a period of unrivaled hypocrisy, immorality in politics and business, and the degradation of democracy; though society was glittery on top, it was hollow and rotting at its core. Perhaps the result of unrealistic hopes after the civil war, there was a massive sense of disappointment with the trajectory of American society as rich got richer as poor got poorer/northerners let the south go its own way, with detrimental results for blacks and poor white farmers/the west was pillaged by farmers and the army.

Social Darwinism: The extrapolation of Darwin’s theory of evolution to the social sphere was an important intellectual development of the Gilded Age. A new set of values emerged in this post-Civil War period, which was characterized by the combination of social Darwinism with the gospel of wealth. According to this school of thought, survival of the fittest was such that the best-qualified people would survive and prosper while everyone else disappeared; the result of this process was considered both natural and divinely ordained. This was, of course, a challenge to the democratic ideal that all people are created equal and should participate in rule-making, which connects to the historical tension between democracy and conservatism (taking social Darwinism as a fundamental premise of conservatism).  Indeed, in the 1870s and 80s, the priority of traditional democratic ideas was reversed: economic goals became the end rather than the means and democracy became conservative because the existing distribution of wealth was taken as a natural process in which human cannot interfere.

Sumner: Sociology (1881)

Background: William Graham Sumner attended and taught at Yale in the second half of the 19th century. He is famous for translating Darwin’s ideas about natural selection into social theory. Sumner considered “fitness” to be one’s material success or wealth; those who did the best economically were at the top of the evolutionary chain. Inequality was a way of ordering society according to natural hierarchies- he thought that property ownership was a sign of superiority so any attempt to restrict that ownership was to interfere in the cosmic evolutionary experience. His vision of society h ad no place for ideals like charity or brotherhood—self interest and wealth were the ruling ideas. Sumner drew much of his inspiration from Thomas Malthus; he thought that the man to land ratio was the determination of everything in social life. In fact, Sumner eventually abandoned a Darwinian explanation for social life and instead argued that because humans have free choice, we are different from other species, and it is only the man/land ratio and economic facts that determine the conditions of our lives. Freedom, for Sumner, is the state in which man can unabashedly pursue his own self-interest without any external intrusion.

Bellamy: Looking Backward (1888)

Background: In reaction to the cynical view of society presented by authors like Sumner, two trends emerged. First was philanthropy- rich men like Carnegie and Stanford believed that their wealth came from processes outside of their control and that they needed to give back to society however they could. Second was the counter-trend of reform Darwinism. Edward Bellamy was the best-known reform Darwinist. His book, Looking Backward, was hugely successful and catalyzed the creation of nationalist clubs, which later became the prototype for political action movements in the late 1890s and early 1900s. His book had huge appeal because it demonstrated what people thought they were missing in late 19th century America.

Looking Backward:

(I think this will be an easily identifiable one because it is written as a first-person narrative, but here’s a basic plot summaryâ€¦)

This is a utopian novel that tells the story of Julian West, a Boston aristocrat who is hypnotized in 1887 and then is discovered in the year 2000 by a man named Dr. Leete. In the Boston of 2000, the economy is based on public ownership of capital and the means of production, and the government event distributes its revenues among citizens. The body of laborers is called the “industrial army” and industrial service spans from age 21 to 24. As Dr. Leete explains, “every man for himself in accordance with his natural aptitude, the utmost pains being taken to enable him to find out what his natural aptitude really is” (133). Great effort is made to equalize the attraction of all the trades. Emphasis is shifted entirely from individualism to the community- this is evident from 21st century innovations like a common umbrella that replaces individual umbrellas (192-3). As Leete explains, “the solidarity of the race and the brotherhood of man, which to you were but fine phrases, are, to our thinking and feeling, ties as real and as vital as physical fraternity” (179).

At one point, Julian has a nightmare that his awakening in 2000 was but a dream. Upon return to 1887, he sees his world for what it is and tries to convince those around him that society can be bettered. They don’t buy it, and Julian wakes up from his nightmare and is overjoyed to find that his transportation to the year 2000 was indeed real.

Veblen: The Theory of the Leisure Class chapters 1-4, 6-7

Chapter 1:  Introductory

Defining the term “leisure class” and then reciting the stages of culture which make a leisure class possible.

“The leisure class as a whole compromises the noble and priestly classes, together with much of their retinue.  The occupations of the class are correspondingly diversified; but they have the common economic characteristic of being non-industrial.” (1)

“When the community passes from peaceable savagery to a predatory phase of life, the conditions of emulation change.  The opportunity and the incentive to emulation increases greatly in scope and urgency.  The activity of men more and more takes on the character of exploit; and an invidious comparison of one hunter or warrior with another grows continually easier and more habitual.” (10)

“Labor acquires a character of irksomeness by virtue of the indignity imputed to it.” (10)

“Predation cannot become the habitual,  conventional resource of any group...until industrial methods have been developed to such a degree of efficiency as to leave a margin worth fighting for, above the subsistence of those engaged in getting a living.” (12)

Chapter 2: Pecuniary Emulation

Importance of ownership and its many manifestations in culture.

“In the sequences of cultural evolution the emergence of a leisure class coincides with the beginning of ownership.  This is necessarily the case, for these two institutions result from the same set of economic forces.  In the inchoate phase of their development, they are but different aspects of the same general facts of social structure.” (15)

“Where the institution of private property is found... the economic process bears the character of a struggle between men for the possession of goods.” (15)

“The possession of wealth confers honour; it is an invidious distinction.” (17)

“Prowess and exploit may still remain the basis award of the highest popular esteem, although the possession of wealth has become the basis of commonplace reputability and of a blameless social standing.  The predatory instinct and the consequent approbation of predatory efficiency are deeply ingrained in the habits of thought of those peoples who have passed under the discipline of a protracted predatory culture.” (19)

Chapter 3: Conspicuous Leisure

Leisure as the “booty” prize of the strongest class; discusses how manners and decorum become the marks of wealth and honor because of their ties to an ability to be idle.

“For this class also [the leisure class] the incentive to diligence and thrift is not absent; but its action is so greatly qualified by the secondary demands of pecuniary emulation, that any inclination in this direction is practically overborne and any incentive to diligence tends to be of no effect.  The most imperative of these secondary demands of emulation, as well as one of the widest scope, is the requirement of abstention from productive work.” (24)

“...a life of leisure is the readiest and most conclusive evidence of pecuniary strength...since application to productive labor is a mark of poverty and subjection, it becomes inconsistent with a reputable standing in the community.” (25)

“What it constitutes [leisure] is non-productive consumption of time.  Time is consumed non-productively (1) from a sense of the unworthiness of productive work, and (2) as an evidence of pecuniary ability to afford a life of idleness.” (28)

Chapter 4: Conspicuous Consumption

Consumption as another outlet for the predatory nature and proof of pecuniary prowess, specifically for women.

“Unproductive consumption of goods is honourable, primarily as a mark of prowess and a prerequisite of human dignity; secondarily it becomes substantially honorable in itself, especially the consumption of the more desirable things.” (44)

This is one of two lectures that we read for class that came from a lecture series James gave at Radcliffe called “Talks for Teachers.” You’ll notice the diction to be a little less scholarly than that found in his other works, although not drastically so. In these two talks, James argues that since we can never completely understand what goes on in someone else’s head, we can never completely understand that person. Due to this inability to communicate, James argues for understanding—meaning we should try our best to understand the circumstances from which someone else comes—and also “hands off”—meaning that since we can never completely get there, we should never presume to be able to regulate someone else’s beliefs or actions.

Lecture 11: John Dewey

“Philosophy and Democracy”

Philosophy, Dewey says, has concerned itself with finding some deeper truth than that which is available through empirical methods. Philosophy’s claim to uncover the Truth underlies its historical support for conservative social orders. Defenders of absolute authority and social hierarchy often appealed to the values and truths supposedly uncovered by philosophy to justify their position. However, in a democracy, philosophy

will construe liberty as meaning a universe in which there is real uncertainty and contingency, a world which is not all in, and never will be, a world which in some respect is incomplete and in the making, and which in these respects may be made this way or that according as men judge, prize, love and labor. To such a philosophy any notion of a perfect or complete reality, finished, existing always the same without regard to the vicissitudes of time, will be abhorrent (207).

A philosophy guided by the progress of science will always keep the door open for new truths. Dewey redefines philosophy as a practice that must be pragmatic. It must suggest new responses to new forms of knowledge.

This essay is significant on many fronts. It is another articulation of “pragmatism,” which we saw earlier in William James. Truth is some that is constantly in flux. Dewey’s support for philosophical experimentation connects with his idea of experimenting in education, of giving full opportunities for the child to develop.

The best way to distinguish a passage from this essay from the rest of Dewey’s essays is to pay attention to any mention of the status, purpose or methodology of philosophy. Dewey does not explicitly give any hard criterion for evaluating truth (whether it “pays” to believe in a certain proposition), but he does state that “[a]ll knowledge in short makes a difference”, that there are practical effects to believing in certain ideas over others.

“The Ethics of Democracy”

In this essay, Dewey makes the bold claim that aristocracy and democracy have the same ends: the realization of individual personality. Dewey debunks the notion that democracy is about unfettered individualism; rather, it is a “form of moral and spiritual association” (59). The difference democracy and aristocracy (and it is an important one) is the means. Aristocracy wrongly assumes that personality can be imposed from on high, that individuals can be given their roles. Conversely, in democracy, each individual is given the freedom to develop his personality.

Dewey’s idea of democracy is distinct from James’. Its promise is not an “intellectual republic” where individuals voice their differences in the public sphere while maintaining their irreducible differences. Dewey conceives of a more organic relationship between the individual and the polity, where a certain melding occurs between the interests of everyone. Ward suggested in section that a helpful way of thinking about the difference between the two thinkers is that Dewey is more Hegelian and James is more Kantian.

“Intelligence and Morals”

Dewey faults the Greek not for subjecting the individual to the state, but for subjecting  “both the state and the individual to an external cosmic order” (65). The idea of a summum bonum, or ‘ultimate good’, lies at the bottom of a misguided moral philosophy. Dewey thinks that the rational sciences can be used to discover what are the uniquely human capacities and desires, and to device ways to cultivate and satisfy them. While this approach to moral philosophy may take on a utilitarian form, Dewey warns against the idea that “‘happiness’ is any less unique than the individuals who experience it” (74). This approach to morality is that same as his approach to democracy, where individual personality is incommensurable.

“If the business is not to search for the one separate moral motive, it is to converge all the instrumentalities of the social arts, of law, education, economics and political science upon the construction of intelligent methods of improving the common lot” (74).

Dewey thinks human behavior is best modified by changing the conditions in which they act, not dictating new principles for action: “In the end, men do what they can do. They refrain from doing what they cannot do” (74). The focus should less be on what is “right” or “wrong” but on how we want people to behave, and how we can change the social environment to induce that behavior.

This essay is another example of the application of pragmatic thought, this time to moral philosophy and social policy. Dewey agrees with James that sources of good for individuals are pluralistic; there is no ‘ultimate good’. Pragmatism allows us to dispense with hard rules and instead judge and bring about different forms of morality by the outcomes we find desirable.

“The Democratic Conception in Education”

In spelling out his conception of democratic education, he agrees with Plato that education should help train individuals in ways that make them useful to others. Education is meant to train our natural aptitudes. Yet Dewey, once again (like he does in “The Ethics of Democracy”), faults Plato for assuming that individuals have fixed natures that determine how they should be educated. When this is assumed, Dewey suggests, there is a loss of diversity of human types: “progress in knowledge has made us aware of the superficiality of Plato’s lumping of individuals and their original powers into a few sharply marked-off classes; it has taught us that original capacities are indefinitely numerous and variable” (113).

Dewey also finds wanting the Enlightenment ideal of the infinite perfectibility of the individual according to the ideas of Nature. Giving free reign to individual development in Nature was “to negate the very idea of education; it was to trust to the accidents of circumstance” (115).

The Germans, in turn, sought to educate their citizens so as to ensure a strong state. Competent, disciplined citizens could defend the state. The German (i.e. Hegelian) view: “The educational process was taken to be one of disciplinary training rather than personal developmentâ€¦.The individual in his isolation is nothing; only in and through an absorption of the aim and meaning of organized institutions does he attain true personality” (117).

Dewey sees a tension between a form of education run by the state and the type of open, cosmopolitan-humanitarian ethos it should try to instill in its citizens.
